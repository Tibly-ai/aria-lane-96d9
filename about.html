The Voice Behind the Mic

Every singer has a story. Here's mine.

Before the stages, the weddings, and the festival lights, there was a kid in Portland singing along to her grandmother's record player. Ten years of professional performance later, that kid still shows up every time I step to the microphone.

"My grandmother's records taught me that a song is a conversation — you just have to mean every word."
My Background

From a record player in Portland to stages across the country

I grew up in Southeast Portland in a house where music was never background noise. My grandmother, who raised me alongside my parents, kept a shelf of vinyl that ran from Ella Fitzgerald to Aretha Franklin to Nina Simone, and she played them like they were scripture. I learned to sing by imitation first — copying every bend, every breath, every held note — long before I knew what any of it was called.

At nineteen, I talked my way into a Tuesday-night slot at a jazz room on Alberta Street. I was terrified, underpaid, and completely hooked. Those early years — three sets a night, learning to read a room that wasn't obligated to listen — taught me more about performance than any classroom could. If you can win over a distracted bar crowd at 11 p.m., you can sing anywhere.

"Technique gets you through the song. Honesty gets you into the room."

The wedding and events work grew naturally from there. A bride heard me at a supper club and asked if I'd sing her ceremony; her photographer passed my name along; and word of mouth did the rest. A decade on, I've performed over 350 shows — from candlelit chapels in the Willamette Valley to corporate galas in Seattle to festival stages in front of thousands — and I still prepare for each one like it's my Tuesday-night audition.

These days I'm based in Portland, Oregon, and travel nationwide. When I'm not performing, I'm usually arranging new material with my trio, digging through crates at record shops, or teaching an occasional vocal workshop for young singers finding their own voice.

Vocal Style

Warm, versatile, and built to serve the song

People often ask what "kind" of singer I am. The honest answer: the kind the song needs. But here's what you can count on in every performance.

♩

A Soulful Core

My natural home is soul and R&B — a warm, resonant tone with grit when the song calls for it and restraint when it doesn't. Think velvet with an edge.

✦

Jazz Sensibility

Years in jazz rooms gave me an ear for phrasing, improvisation, and space. I don't just sing the melody — I have a conversation with it.

♪

Contemporary Range

I move comfortably into modern pop and acoustic ballads, reinterpreting current hits so they feel timeless rather than borrowed.

◈

Dynamic Control

From a near-whisper during your vows to a full-belt finale that fills a festival field — I shape volume and intensity to the moment, not the other way around.

For the technically curious: I'm a mezzo-soprano with a working range of roughly three octaves (F3–F6), comfortable in both chest-dominant soul belting and airy head-voice balladry.

Training & Craft

The work behind the voice

Natural instinct started the journey, but craft sustains it. Here's how the training came together — and how it continues today.

Ages 8–18

Choir, Piano & First Fundamentals

A decade in the Portland Youth Chorale and weekly piano lessons built my foundation: sight-reading, harmony, breath support, and the discipline of rehearsal. Choir taught me to blend; solos taught me to lead.

Ages 18–22

Classical Voice Study, Portland State University

Four years of classical vocal training in PSU's music program — bel canto technique, diction in four languages, and vocal health practices that let me sing five-hour wedding receptions without losing a note the next day.

Ages 19–25

The Jazz Room Apprenticeship

My real graduate school: hundreds of live sets in Portland's jazz and supper clubs, mentored by veteran bandleader Curtis Hale, who drilled me on standards, scatting, and the art of listening to a band instead of just singing over one.

Ongoing

Continuous Coaching & Study

I still take monthly sessions with a contemporary vocal coach, attend an annual intensive on speech-level singing, and maintain a daily warm-up practice. Instruments need tuning; so do voices.

Artistic Influences

The voices that shaped mine

You'll hear echoes of these artists in my sound — not imitations, but lessons absorbed over thousands of hours of listening.

Etta James Emotional Truth

Etta taught me that a great vocal isn't polite. Her willingness to sound raw — to let the ache show — is the standard I hold myself to on every ballad, especially first dances.

Ella Fitzgerald Precision & Play

The first voice from my grandmother's shelf. Ella's impossible clarity and joyful improvisation shaped my phrasing and my belief that technical mastery should sound effortless.

Nina Simone Interpretation

Nina showed me a singer can completely reinvent a song and make it more true than the original. That's the spirit behind every reimagined pop cover in my sets.

Adele Modern Storytelling

Proof that big, honest, unadorned singing still stops a room in the streaming era. Her work informs how I bridge classic soul with the songs today's audiences know by heart.

Frank Sinatra Command of a Room

From Sinatra I learned that the space between the notes matters, and that a performer's real instrument is the audience's attention. Essential study for galas and supper clubs.

Amy Winehouse Fearless Blending

Amy fused jazz phrasing with contemporary writing in a way that felt inevitable. She gave me permission to stop choosing between genres and start weaving them together.
